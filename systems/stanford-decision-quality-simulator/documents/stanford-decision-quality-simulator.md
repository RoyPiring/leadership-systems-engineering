## Building a Stanford-Grade Decision Simulator

### Project vision and purpose

This project builds a structured decision simulator designed to apply Stanford GSB’s Decision Quality framework and Pfeffer’s Power framework to high-stakes business scenarios.

The system is built to move decision-making away from instinct-only reasoning and toward measurable decision quality. Instead of evaluating whether an outcome was successful after the fact, the simulator evaluates whether the decision process itself was structurally sound before execution begins. The focus is on framing, information quality, alternatives, trade-offs, reasoning, and organizational commitment under realistic operational pressure.

The simulator also introduces power dynamics as an execution layer. A technically correct strategy can still fail if stakeholder alignment, influence, and sequencing are ignored. The project combines both frameworks to evaluate not just what decision should be made, but whether the organization can realistically execute it.

### Workspace setup goals

The environment establishes the simulation, teaching, and evaluation layers.

A dedicated workspace is created and connected to Claude Cowork mode to organize all scenario files, templates, and framework references into a centralized system. Six project files are generated to support structured execution, including scoring templates, reference cards, and simulation prompts.

The NorthernTech AI transformation scenario acts as the primary case study. Before applying any framework, an initial instinct-based prediction is recorded to create a measurable comparison between gut judgment and framework-driven reasoning later in the simulation.

This setup ensures that the decision process can be observed step by step rather than evaluated only by its final recommendation.

![Image](https://learn.nextwork.org/refreshed_maroon_timid_jujube/uploads/79347397-b29f-4b0b-afa3-d089c3e1ff9d_qxjwtg8s)

### Pre-framework gut prediction

The initial instinct favored a PARTNER strategy.

The reasoning was based primarily on speed. NorthernTech was already 18 months behind in AI adoption, and a partnership path appeared faster than either building internally or attempting a full acquisition. The prediction also assumed that acquiring another company could introduce integration risks and potentially lead to talent loss during consolidation.

However, the prediction was intentionally labeled as incomplete because it lacked structured scoring, governance analysis, and operational constraints. The purpose of recording the instinct early was to later compare how structured decision analysis changes both confidence and implementation planning.

## Mastering the DQ Six Elements and Power Frameworks

### Framework learning objectives

The simulation introduces the six Decision Quality elements as the core scoring structure.

Frame, Alternatives, Information, Values and Trade-offs, Reasoning, and Commitment are treated as independent evaluation layers rather than a blended average. The framework uses weakest-link scoring, meaning the overall decision quality is capped by the lowest-scoring element regardless of strengths elsewhere.

This changes how decisions are improved. Instead of optimizing already strong areas, effort must focus on repairing structural weaknesses that reduce the overall integrity of the decision.

The project also introduces Pfeffer’s 7 Rules of Power as an execution overlay. These rules analyze influence, stakeholder alignment, sequencing, and organizational behavior to determine whether a technically sound strategy can survive real-world implementation pressures.

![Image](https://learn.nextwork.org/refreshed_maroon_timid_jujube/uploads/79347397-b29f-4b0b-afa3-d089c3e1ff9d_9fsv51wm)

### DQ elements recall and weakest-link logic

The weakest-link model fundamentally changes how decision quality is measured.

A decision with five strong elements and one weak element is still considered a weak decision overall because failure usually occurs at the weakest structural point. A 95 across most dimensions still collapses operationally if one critical area, such as Information or Commitment, scores poorly.

This approach prevents false confidence created by averaging. Instead of hiding weaknesses behind strong categories, the simulator forces direct confrontation of the lowest-quality element first.

The framework also reinforces that decision quality is separate from outcome quality. A strong process can still lead to a bad outcome due to uncertainty, while a weak process can occasionally produce a good outcome by luck.

## Applying DQ Frameworks to a Real Decision

### Warm-up exercise goals

The simulation applies the framework to a real pending decision before entering the larger NorthernTech scenario.

The objective is to observe how a seemingly straightforward decision changes once the six DQ elements are scored individually. The exercise demonstrates how information gaps, assumptions, and operational realities can materially alter both confidence and implementation strategy.

The Power overlay is introduced simultaneously to show how influence and organizational dynamics affect execution independently from strategic correctness.

![Image](https://learn.nextwork.org/refreshed_maroon_timid_jujube/uploads/79347397-b29f-4b0b-afa3-d089c3e1ff9d_0hxfojo4)

### Identifying the weakest DQ element

The weakest element was identified as Information.

A key operational constraint had not been validated early in the process, specifically the Leidos conflict-of-interest status and how it would affect government-sector opportunities. This missing information represented a potentially decision-altering factor that had not yet been confirmed.

Once identified, the information gap reframed the decision because it directly affected execution feasibility rather than abstract strategy. This reinforced a central principle of the framework: the most important information is often operational and discovered late unless explicitly surfaced during analysis.

The exercise also highlighted that execution teams frequently hold the most decision-relevant information, not executive stakeholders.

## Making the $400M NorthernTech AI Pivot Decision

### Live decision exercise objectives

The primary simulation focuses on NorthernTech’s AI transformation strategy under competitive pressure.

The objective is to frame the problem correctly, generate alternatives beyond the default options, evaluate information quality, and score all six DQ dimensions under operational constraints. Rather than simply selecting Build, Buy, or Partner, the exercise forces examination of sequencing, timing, governance, and execution dependencies.

The framework encourages exploring hybrid approaches rather than treating strategic choices as mutually exclusive.

![Image](https://learn.nextwork.org/refreshed_maroon_timid_jujube/uploads/79347397-b29f-4b0b-afa3-d089c3e1ff9d_7w2244kg)

### Weakest-link DQ score and analysis

The final weakest-link score was 75 out of 100.

The lowest-scoring element was Frame, tied closely with Alternatives. The framing lacked sufficient specificity around timeline, target customer segment, assumptions, and falsifiability conditions. While the overall strategic direction was reasonable, the framing did not yet define enough measurable constraints to qualify as high-quality decision architecture.

The analysis demonstrated that even a generally correct strategic direction can remain structurally weak if the framing lacks precision.

This also reinforced the difference between having an answer and having a defensible decision process.

## Layering Pfeffer's Power Rules onto the Decision

### Power overlay objectives

The Power framework evaluates whether the chosen strategy can survive organizational execution.

Rather than changing the strategic answer directly, the framework changes sequencing, communication patterns, stakeholder engagement, and relationship management. It focuses on where resistance, influence gaps, or political friction could cause the strategy to fail operationally.

This introduces a second layer of realism into the simulation because many organizational failures occur after the decision itself is technically correct.

![Image](https://learn.nextwork.org/refreshed_maroon_timid_jujube/uploads/79347397-b29f-4b0b-afa3-d089c3e1ff9d_qzws0x1w)

### How power dynamics shifted the decision

The Power overlay preserved the PARTNER-to-BUILD strategy but significantly changed implementation sequencing.

Internal communication became a priority before external negotiation to prevent engineering talent loss during the partnership phase. Key board stakeholders also needed alignment before formal meetings to reduce resistance during approval discussions.

A major execution risk also emerged around relationship access. Negotiating with VectorFlow’s leadership through cold outreach created competitive vulnerability because multiple partnership discussions were already active.

Without accounting for power dynamics, the strategic recommendation likely would have failed despite being directionally correct.

## Passing the Board Director Standards Gate

### Governance review objectives

The governance review evaluates whether the decision can survive board-level scrutiny.

This includes validating financial assumptions, documenting major risks, defining measurable milestones, and identifying ESG or reputational concerns. The review process pushes the decision from operational plausibility toward governance-grade defensibility.

The framework treats governance not as bureaucracy but as another quality filter.

### Raising the weakest element above 70

The weakest element already exceeded the minimum threshold, so no structural rework was required.

However, governance improvements were still added, including quantified risks, mitigation plans, milestone metrics, and broader reputation analysis. Additional refinements that could raise the Frame score further included tighter customer segmentation, explicit assumptions, and clearer falsifiability conditions.

This demonstrated that governance review strengthens rigor even when baseline thresholds are already met.

## Teaching Back and Committing to Real-World Transfer

### Debrief and transfer objectives

The final phase validates whether the framework has been internalized.

An After-Action Review compares the original instinct against the framework-driven result to identify where reasoning changed. A teach-back exercise forces explanation of both frameworks from memory, ensuring understanding goes beyond passive familiarity.

The simulation then transitions into real-world application by requiring a live decision commitment with measurable execution milestones.

This closes the loop between simulation and operational behavior.

![Image](https://learn.nextwork.org/refreshed_maroon_timid_jujube/uploads/79347397-b29f-4b0b-afa3-d089c3e1ff9d_qpaqne0a)

### The element that most changed my thinking

Information had the greatest impact on the final decision.

Operational realities surfaced by execution-focused stakeholders materially changed implementation sequencing and strategic structure. This demonstrated that high-value decision inputs frequently emerge from people closest to execution rather than from abstract strategic modeling.

The framework also reinforced that operational constraints are not secondary details. They often determine whether a strategy is actually viable.

![Image](https://learn.nextwork.org/refreshed_maroon_timid_jujube/uploads/79347397-b29f-4b0b-afa3-d089c3e1ff9d_lpgk9ts6)

### 7-day real decision commitment

The simulation concludes with a real operational commitment tied to measurable execution steps.

The selected decision focused on validating a productized AI Readiness Audit offering as a go-to-market strategy. Success criteria were defined through concrete deliverables, including creating a one-pager, building a target network list, and initiating outreach conversations.

This step transforms the framework from theoretical analysis into an operational discipline tied to accountability and measurable progress.

## Secret Mission: Proving the Method Generalizes

![Image](https://learn.nextwork.org/refreshed_maroon_timid_jujube/uploads/79347397-b29f-4b0b-afa3-d089c3e1ff9d_pyde4qf8)

### Patterns noticed across both scenario runs

The Power framework consistently affected implementation more than strategic direction.

In both simulations, the core recommendation remained largely intact while execution sequencing, stakeholder alignment, and relationship management changed substantially.

The DQ framework also showed uneven improvement across elements. Frame, Information, and Commitment improved more predictably across scenarios, while Alternatives improved more slowly because divergent thinking depends heavily on scenario-specific creativity.

A key insight was that repeated framework use began influencing intuition itself. The second scenario showed stronger gut prediction structure before formal analysis even started.

## Reflections and Key Takeaways

### Tools and concepts mastered

This project uses a three-persona simulator, Claude Desktop with Cowork mode, the Decision Quality framework, Pfeffer’s 7 Rules of Power, and a structured operating system for decision-making.

The core concepts include weakest-link scoring, strategic framing, operational information analysis, governance evaluation, power dynamics, and decision transfer into real-world execution.

The simulator treats decisions as systems that can be analyzed, stress-tested, and improved systematically rather than relying only on instinct.

### Time and challenges

This project took approximately 90 minutes.

The most challenging part was committing to a real-world operational decision and scheduling measurable follow-up actions. Translating frameworks into actual accountability created more resistance than the analytical portions of the simulation.

### Personal learning reflection

This project focused on learning how to apply the Decision Quality framework and Pfeffer’s Power framework to complex strategic decisions.

The next step is strengthening project and operational management capabilities so that structured decision-making translates more effectively into execution across larger systems and teams.
