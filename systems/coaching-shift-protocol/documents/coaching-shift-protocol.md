## Why This Project: The Conversation That Never Changes

### The stuck person and the real-world motivation

In this project, I built a coaching workflow system inside Obsidian using Claude Code and Claude Desktop App. The goal was to create a repeatable framework for guiding conversations from emotional resistance into clear action using structured coaching techniques.

The system combines AI-assisted coaching simulations, energy assessments, structured protocols, and session scoring into a single operational workspace. Instead of treating coaching as abstract theory, the project focused on building a process that can be practiced, measured, and improved over time.

## Setting Up the Coaching Skills Environment

### What this step accomplishes

I configured Claude Code CLI with an Obsidian vault named shift-protocol to create a shared working environment between the AI system and my local documentation workspace.

This setup allowed coaching sessions, protocols, assessments, and reflections to exist as structured artifacts instead of temporary conversations. The environment functions as both a thinking system and a persistent operational record.

![Image](https://learn.nextwork.org/refreshed_maroon_timid_jujube/uploads/09106b26-f25c-425c-a10b-fcf83293c90f_od2dhc46)

### Claude Code and Obsidian working together

Claude Code operated directly inside the Obsidian vault, allowing files to be created, edited, and organized in real time.

This created a workflow where Claude handled structured generation and refinement while Obsidian served as the long-term knowledge layer. The result was a shared filesystem model where coaching artifacts could evolve continuously instead of being recreated from scratch.

## Building the Shift Protocol Vault

### Scaffolding the artifact system

The vault was structured into assessments, sessions, protocols, transfer workflows, templates, and prompts.

This organization separates operational data from reusable frameworks, making the system easier to maintain and scale. Each folder serves a specific role within the coaching lifecycle, allowing sessions to move from live interaction into reusable knowledge and measurable progress.

### The three personas and their roles

The system uses three distinct personas to simulate a complete coaching environment.

The Master Coach Mentor teaches and evaluates the coaching process. Marcus acts as the live coachee with resistance patterns and emotional triggers. The ICF/iPEC Standards Gate functions as the quality-control layer that scores coaching behavior against professional coaching competencies.

This separation creates role clarity inside the workflow and prevents the interaction from collapsing into generic AI conversation.

## Establishing Your Energy Baseline: Self-ELI Assessment

### What the assessment reveals

I completed a structured Self-ELI assessment to measure baseline energy patterns under normal and stress conditions.

The assessment exposed where emotional energy is productive versus reactive, providing a measurable starting point before conducting coaching sessions. This establishes self-awareness as part of the operational workflow rather than treating it as intuition alone.

![Image](https://learn.nextwork.org/refreshed_maroon_timid_jujube/uploads/09106b26-f25c-425c-a10b-fcf83293c90f_orc0rrtw)

### Normal energy vs. stress energy patterns

Under normal conditions, my baseline remained centered around Level 4 concern and responsibility. Under stress, energy shifted heavily toward frustration and self-criticism.

The important insight was not the drop itself, but the consistency of the pattern. The stress response repeatedly redirected energy away from execution and into internal resistance. Identifying that loop made it easier to understand how energy affects decision-making and communication during high-pressure situations.

## Mastering Powerful Questions vs. Advice-Giving

### Learning the 4-Step Shift Arc

The project focused heavily on understanding the difference between guiding awareness and giving advice.

The 4-Step Shift Arc created a structure for moving conversations from emotional resistance toward self-generated insight. This shifted the interaction model away from solving problems for people and toward helping them identify the underlying pattern themselves.

![Image](https://learn.nextwork.org/refreshed_maroon_timid_jujube/uploads/09106b26-f25c-425c-a10b-fcf83293c90f_wu88vb8y)

### The critical distinction that changes every conversation

The biggest distinction was recognizing that advice closes exploration while powerful questions expand it.

Advice introduces an external answer. A powerful question forces the person to examine the meaning behind their own words. The system reinforced this by treating certain phrases as “lock-words” that reveal deeper emotional context when explored correctly.

## Conducting a Live Coaching Session with Marcus

### The 30-minute coaching session

I conducted a simulated coaching session with Marcus using only powerful questions and the structured coaching framework.

The objective was to guide the conversation without inserting interpretation or directing the outcome. This tested whether awareness and emotional shifts could emerge naturally through questioning alone.

### The most powerful question and what it unlocked

The most effective question was: “What does your best work mean to you?”

That question exposed a deeper emotional pattern connected to Marcus’s relationship with his father and his fear of becoming disengaged in the same way. The session shifted from frustration with leadership into recognition of a personal identity pattern driving his behavior.

The important takeaway was that the breakthrough did not come from providing insight. It came from creating enough space for the coachee to generate it independently.

## Scoring the Session: ICF Competency Evaluation

### Getting scored against professional coaching standards

The session was evaluated against ICF coaching competencies and energy coaching standards.

This introduced measurable feedback into the workflow, turning the session into a repeatable performance system rather than a subjective conversation. Scores, transcripts, and observations were stored in the vault as long-term evidence artifacts.

### ICF score and strongest competency

The session received a 9/10 score, with the strongest competencies being presence, active listening, and awareness generation.

The highest-scoring moments came from maintaining silence long enough for the coachee to continue processing instead of interrupting with interpretation. This reinforced that restraint is often more valuable than explanation in coaching environments.

![Image](https://learn.nextwork.org/refreshed_maroon_timid_jujube/uploads/09106b26-f25c-425c-a10b-fcf83293c90f_rwi2i7sz)

### Advice-giving patterns and the powerful question alternative

One recurring pattern identified during evaluation was the urge to complete the coachee’s insight with my own framework.

Replacing that instinct with short prompts such as “Keep going” returned ownership of the thought process back to the coachee. This consistently produced stronger awareness and more authentic responses.

## Building a Reusable Protocol and 7-Day Transfer System

### Processing the session into lasting artifacts

The coaching session was converted into reusable operational artifacts including protocols, transfer exercises, and teach-back exercises.

This transformed the interaction from a one-time session into a repeatable system for continued application and skill development.

![Image](https://learn.nextwork.org/refreshed_maroon_timid_jujube/uploads/09106b26-f25c-425c-a10b-fcf83293c90f_kjays6os)

### Turning an advice moment into a powerful question

One of the strongest learning moments came from intentionally resisting the urge to explain or validate Marcus’s insight.

Instead of extending his statement with interpretation, the response “Keep going” created enough silence for him to uncover the deeper realization himself. That moment reinforced that effective coaching depends more on disciplined listening than intellectual contribution.

![Image](https://learn.nextwork.org/refreshed_maroon_timid_jujube/uploads/09106b26-f25c-425c-a10b-fcf83293c90f_1yyin1tr)

### Three real conversations calendared within 7 days

I scheduled three conversations across personal, leadership, and high-pressure scenarios to apply the protocol in real environments.

This moved the framework out of simulation and into live execution, allowing the system to be tested against different emotional dynamics and communication patterns.

## Deploying the Protocol on a Real Person

### Real-world energy shift documented

I applied the coaching framework during a real conversation with my wife around stress and identity changes after parenthood.

The interaction moved from surface-level frustration into a deeper emotional insight about losing connection to a previous version of herself. The shift occurred gradually through targeted questions and extended silence rather than direct advice.

This demonstrated that the protocol works best when emotional ownership remains with the other person.

### Day 1 vs. Day 3 skill progression

Comparing later sessions showed measurable improvement in presence and silence tolerance.

The coaching became less reactive and more observational, especially during emotionally charged moments. The most noticeable growth came from resisting the urge to fill silence prematurely.

## Reflections and What Comes Next

### Tools and concepts mastered

This project combined Claude Code, Claude Desktop App, and Obsidian into a structured coaching workflow system.

The main concepts developed were energy awareness, coaching presence, emotional pattern recognition, and the operational use of powerful questions instead of advice.

### Time investment and challenges

This project took approximately 90 minutes. The most difficult part was resisting the instinct to solve problems instead of guiding awareness.

That shift required learning how to tolerate silence, uncertainty, and incomplete thoughts long enough for the other person to continue processing independently.

### Personal takeaway and next learning goal

The biggest takeaway was understanding that coaching effectiveness comes less from intelligence and more from disciplined presence.

The next area of focus is conflict resolution and navigating emotionally charged conversations without losing structure or awareness.
